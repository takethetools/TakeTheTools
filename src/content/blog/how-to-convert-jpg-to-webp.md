## 1. The Mathematical Foundation: JPG vs. WebP

To understand how JPG translates to WebP, we must first master the concept of **Lossy Compression**. Both formats throw away data that the human eye cannot see, but they do it with very different mathematical philosophies.

### 1.1 The JPEG Legacy: Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)
JPEG works by dividing an image into 8x8 blocks and using "Discrete Cosine Transform" to convert pixel values into frequencies. It then "Quantizes" these frequencies, removing the ones humans don't perceive well. This 30-year-old math is robust but often leads to "Blocky Artifacts" at high compression.

### 1.2 The WebP Innovation: Intra-Prediction
WebP (Lossy) is based on the VP8 video codec's "Intra-frame coding." Instead of just looking at one block, it looks at the blocks to the left and above to "Predict" what the next area should look like. It only saves the "Residual" (the error in prediction). This "Predictive math" is significantly more sophisticated than the static DCT used by JPG.

## 2. A Deep Dive into the Evolution of Photographic Standards

The journey from the "Hard Drive" era to the "Real-Time Web" required a fundamental shift in how we think about photographs.

### 2.1 The JPEG Heritage (1992)
The Joint Photographic Experts Group created the JPEG standard in an era when most computers had only a few megabytes of RAM. It was designed for "Cold Storage"—compact enough to fit on a floppy disk but slow to process. 

### 2.2 The WebP Emergence (2010 onwards)
As Google realized that "Seconds equal Revenue," they developed WebP to solve the "JPG Bottleneck." By repurposing the math from high-speed video streaming, they created a format that could deliver photographic richness at a fraction of the weight, proving that "Video-grade Logic" was the future of static design.

### 2.3 The Web Standards Tipping Point
Today, we use JPG-to-WebP conversion as a "Primary Verification" layer for every high-authority website. If you're not serving WebP, you're essentially leaving your traffic and revenue at the mercy of slower legacy technology.
