## Percentages Are Everywhere — And Frequently Misunderstood

Percentages appear in almost every area of daily life — discounts, tax, salary increases, exam scores, interest rates, statistics, nutritional labels, and more. Most people learned how to calculate percentages in school and promptly forgot the mechanics as soon as they had a calculator available.

The problem is that there are several different types of percentage calculations, and mixing them up leads to wrong answers. "What is 15% of 80?" is a different calculation from "80 is 15% of what?" and both are different from "what percentage increase is it from 80 to 92?"

A percentage calculator handles all of these cases so you get the right answer without thinking through which formula applies.

## The Four Most Common Percentage Calculations

**1. Finding a percentage of a number**

Formula: Result = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Number

Example: What is 20% of 350?
(20 ÷ 100) × 350 = 0.20 × 350 = **70**

Use this for: calculating discounts, finding tax amounts, determining tips.

**2. Finding what percentage one number is of another**

Formula: Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100

Example: 45 is what percent of 180?
(45 ÷ 180) × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = **25%**

Use this for: calculating exam scores, finding what share one value represents of a total.

**3. Calculating percentage increase**

Formula: Percentage increase = ((New − Original) ÷ Original) × 100

Example: A salary increases from Rs 50,000 to Rs 58,000. What is the percentage increase?
((58,000 − 50,000) ÷ 50,000) × 100 = (8,000 ÷ 50,000) × 100 = **16%**

Use this for: salary negotiations, price changes, growth calculations.

**4. Calculating percentage decrease**

Same formula as increase, but the result is negative when the new value is lower.

Example: A product price drops from Rs 2,400 to Rs 1,980. What is the percentage decrease?
((1,980 − 2,400) ÷ 2,400) × 100 = (−420 ÷ 2,400) × 100 = **−17.5%**

The product decreased in price by 17.5%.

## Real-World Examples

**Shopping discounts:**
A jacket is listed at Rs 8,500 with a 30% discount. What is the sale price?
30% of 8,500 = 2,550. Sale price = 8,500 − 2,550 = **Rs 5,950**

**Sales tax:**
A laptop costs Rs 120,000 before 17% sales tax. What is the total?
17% of 120,000 = 20,400. Total = 120,000 + 20,400 = **Rs 140,400**

**Exam score:**
You answered 43 out of 50 questions correctly. What is your percentage score?
(43 ÷ 50) × 100 = **86%**

**Salary negotiation:**
Your current salary is Rs 80,000 and you want a 15% raise. What would your new salary be?
15% of 80,000 = 12,000. New salary = 80,000 + 12,000 = **Rs 92,000**

**Investment return:**
You invested Rs 200,000 and it grew to Rs 247,000. What was your return percentage?
((247,000 − 200,000) ÷ 200,000) × 100 = **23.5%**

## A Common Mistake: Percentage Increase vs Percentage Points

These two concepts are frequently confused and the difference matters.

If an interest rate rises from 5% to 8%, it increased by **3 percentage points** (the arithmetic difference between the two percentages).

But the percentage increase in the interest rate is: ((8 − 5) ÷ 5) × 100 = **60%** (the rate itself increased by 60%).

Both statements are true and both are meaningful in different contexts. "The rate rose by 3 percentage points" and "the rate increased by 60%" describe the same change from different perspectives. Knowing which is being used matters when evaluating financial products, statistics, or economic claims.

## Tip Calculation — The Quick Version

For restaurant tips, a fast mental shortcut:

**10% tip:** Move the decimal point one place left. Rs 1,850 → Rs 185 tip.

**15% tip:** Find 10%, then add half of that. Rs 185 + Rs 92.50 = Rs 277.50 tip.

**20% tip:** Find 10% and double it. Rs 185 × 2 = Rs 370 tip.
