### A Deep Dive into the Evolution of Temperature Measurement

Before the standardized "Degree," humans measured heat by physical markers like the boiling of lead or the freezing of wine. 

- **The Fahrenheit Era:** Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit created the first reliable mercury thermometer and defined his scale based on three points: brine, water/ice, and human body temperature.
- **The Celsius Era:** Anders Celsius proposed a decimal scale where 0 was boiling and 100 was freezing (later reversed). This became the standard for the Metric world.
- **The Kelvin Era:** Lord Kelvin identified the concept of "Absolute Zero"—the point where all molecular motion stops. This scale is the foundation of all modern physics and NASA-level engineering.

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. **Why does the US use Fahrenheit?** It was the primary scale used in the British Empire; while the UK moved to Celsius, the US maintained the tradition for consumer use.
2. **Is Kelvin used for weather?** No, Kelvin is primarily used in science and engineering to avoid negative numbers and represent absolute energy.
3. **How do I convert Celsius to Fahrenheit in my head?** Double the Celsius, subtract 10%, and add 32 (e.g., 20°C -> 40 - 4 + 32 = 68°F).
4. **What is "Standard Temperature"?** In chemistry (STP), it is 0°C (273.15 K).
5. **How does altitude affect the boiling point?** Water boils at a lower temperature at higher altitudes due to lower pressure.
6. **What is the coldest temperature possible?** Absolute Zero (0 Kelvin).
7. **Are there other scales?** Yes, Rankine and Réaumur exist but are rarely used in modern contexts.
8. **Is 40 below the same in both?** Yes, -40°C is exactly equal to -40°F.
9. **How precise is our converter?** We use high-precision floating-point arithmetic suitable for most industrial and scientific applications.
10. **Does humidity affect temperature?** humidity affects "Perceived Temp" (Heat Index), but the base temperature remains the same.
