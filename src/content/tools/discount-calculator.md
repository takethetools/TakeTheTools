## 1. The Mathematical Foundation: Percentage Decrease

To understand how a discount works, we must first look at the **Mathematical Constant of the Multiplier**.

### 1.1 The Discount Percentage Formula
A discount is not just "Subtraction." It is a scaling operation.
- **The Calculation**: $Savings = Original Price \times (Discount Percentage / 100)$.
- **The Final Price**: $Final = Original - Savings$.
Our engine uses high-precision floating-point math to ensure that for an item costing $99.99$ with a $17.5\%$ discount, the resulting price is geographically perfect to the cent.

### 1.2 The "Inverse" Discovery
Sometimes, you know the Sale Price and the Discount, but you need to find the **Original Price**.
- **The Formula**: $Original = Sale Price / (1 - (Discount / 100))$.
This "Reverse Calculation" is a vital asset for price-auditing and verifying the honesty of "Marked Down" items in a retail environment.

## 2. A Deep Dive into "Compound Discounts" (Stacking)

The biggest challenge in consumer finance is the **Stackable Coupon Trap**.

### 2.1 The Non-Linearity of Multi-Stage Savings
If you have a $50\%$ off sale and use an additional $50\%$ off coupon, the item is *not* free.
- **The Math**:
  - Stage 1: $100 \times 0.5 = 50$.
  - Stage 2: $50 \times 0.5 = 25$.
- **The Result**: You have a total discount of $75\%$, not $100\%$.

## 8. Historical Anecdotes: The Rise of the "Price Tag"
Before the 1870s, there were no "Discounts" because there were no "Fixed Prices." Every purchase was a result of **Haggling** in a bazaar or general store. The "Fixed Price" was popularized by **John Wanamaker** in Philadelphia, who introduced the "Return Policy" and the "Mark Down." This "Strategic Retail Revolution" created the concept of a "Sale," transforming the world from "Bartering" into the high-performance consumer economy we have today.
