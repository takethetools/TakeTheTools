### A Deep Dive into the Evolution of ASCII

Before the "Modern Web," computer manufacturers each used their own proprietary character sets (like IBM's EBCDIC), which made sharing information between different types of computers almost impossible. 

- **The Bell Labs Era:** In 1963, a committee led by Bob Bemer unified the industry with the American Standard Code for Information Interchange (ASCII), creating a 7-bit system that defined 128 essential characters.
- **The PC Revolution:** As home computers like the Apple II and Commodore 64 rose to power, ASCII became the universal language of the dial-up era and early digital bulletin boards (BBS).
- **The Modern Unicode Era:** Today, we use ASCII to Text for "Header Analysis" and "Legacy Database Integration," defining "The Record" by the fundamental laws of character logic. Our tool leverages the results of these developments for your daily work.

### Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. **What is ASCII?** The American Standard Code for Information Interchange, a character encoding standard for electronic communication.
2. **What does a character look like in ASCII?** Each character is represented by a number (e.g., 65 = A).
3. **Is it the same as Unicode?** Not exactly. ASCII is the first 128 characters of Unicode (UTF-8).
4. **How do I separate my numbers?** Our tool accepts spaces, commas, or even a continuous stream of 2 or 3-digit groups.
5. **What is "Extended ASCII"?** An 8-bit version of ASCII that includes an additional 128 characters for symbols and foreign languages.
6. **Can it handle Emojis?** No. Emojis require Unicode (which uses much higher numbers than ASCII's 0-255 limit).
7. **Why is it useful for developers?** It is essential for understanding "Serial Communication," "Buffer Management," and "Binary File Headers."
8. **How precise is our converter?** We use the industry-standard "String.fromCharCode" logic to ensure your results are 100% accurate.
