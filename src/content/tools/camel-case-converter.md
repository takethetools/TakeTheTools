## 1. The Mathematical Foundation: Tokenization and Regex

To understand how text becomes "Camel Case," we must first look at the **Lexical Tokenization** used to identify word boundaries.

### 1.1 The "Word Boundary" Pattern
A string like `this is a variable` must be broken into individual "Tokens" before it can be re-cased.
- **The Pattern**: `[A-Za-z0-9]+`
Our engine identifies any non-alphanumeric character (spaces, underscores, dashes) as a "Separator." It isolates the words `this`, `is`, `a`, and `variable`, preparing them for the "Recasing Pipeline."

### 1.2 The Recursive Joining Logic
Once tokenized, the math of camelCase is simple but strict:
1. **Lower Camel Case (camelCase)**: The first token is forced to lowercase. Every subsequent token has its first letter capitalized.
2. **Upper Camel Case (PascalCase)**: *Every* token has its first letter capitalized.
Our tool utilizes high-performance string manipulation algorithms to perform these transformations in constant O(n) time, making it suitable for massive data streams.

## 8. Historical Anecdotes: The "Xerox Alto" Origin
The term "Camel Case" didn't exist in the early days of programming. The convention was originally called "BumpyCase" or "InnerCaps." It was popularized at **Xerox PARC** in the 1970s for the **Mesa** programming language used on the **Xerox Alto**—the world's first modern personal computer. This history proves that "Naming Strategy" has always been at the heart of the global computing revolution.
